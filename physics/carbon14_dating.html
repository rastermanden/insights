Decay equations
\[ N_{C}(t) = N_0\, e^{-\lambda t} \qquad N_{N}(t) = N_0\!\left(1 - e^{-\lambda t}\right) \] \[ t = \frac{\ln(N_0 / N_C)}{\lambda} = \frac{t_{1/2}}{\ln 2}\,\ln\!\frac{N_0}{N_C} \]

Why Carbon-14? Living organisms continuously exchange carbon with the atmosphere, maintaining a fixed ratio of radioactive C-14 to stable C-12. When an organism dies, this exchange stops and the C-14 decays into nitrogen-14 (N-14) via beta emission — the clock starts. Measuring how much C-14 remains tells us how long ago the organism died.

Half-life: C-14 has a half-life of 5,730 years, making it ideal for dating organic materials up to about 50,000 years old — from ancient charcoal and wooden artifacts to bones, seeds, and parchment.

Reading the chart: The blue curve shows the C-14 fraction remaining over time. The red curve shows the N-14 that has accumulated. The green vertical line marks the age calculated from the measured fraction, showing where the sample sits on both curves.
