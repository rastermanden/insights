Decay equations
\[ N_{K}(t) = N_{K_0}\, e^{-\lambda t} \qquad N_{Ar}(t) = N_{K_0}\,\frac{\lambda_{Ar}}{\lambda}\!\left(e^{\lambda t} - 1\right)e^{-\lambda t} \] \[ t = \frac{1}{\lambda}\ln\!\left(1 + \frac{\lambda}{\lambda_{Ar}}\cdot\frac{N_{Ar}}{N_K}\right) \]

Why K-40 and Ar-40? When magma cools and solidifies into rock, any pre-existing argon gas escapes — the clock is reset to zero. From that moment, the radioactive isotope potassium-40 (K-40) decays. About 10.5 % of decays produce argon-40 (Ar-40), which is trapped in the crystal lattice. The remaining 89.5 % produce calcium-40 (not shown).

Half-life: K-40 has a half-life of 1.248 billion years, making it ideal for dating rocks millions to billions of years old — from young volcanic flows to ancient continental crust and even meteorites.

Reading the chart: The blue curve shows how much K-40 remains as a fraction of the original amount. The red curve shows the build-up of Ar-40 (as a fraction of N₀). The green vertical line marks the sample's measured Ar-40 / K-40 ratio, and where it crosses the curves shows the current state of the sample.
